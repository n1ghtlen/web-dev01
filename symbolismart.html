History of Symbolism

Symbolism was an artistic movement most prominent in the late 19th century in France. The style is based on a dreamy, fantastical take of reality and was unsurprisingly the antithesis to realism eras like Impressionism. Like the name suggests, symbols are often used to portay the inner world of the subject or the artist.

Objective reality does not exist in these pieces, but rather an emotional reaction to industrialism and its changing impact on society. Not only painters, but also poets and other literary artists expressed their innermost feelings.

Pieces often express the idea that there is more beyond that which is physical, and there is a story to be told in the context of the spiritual world and figures that represent that world.

Famous Artists

Gustav Klimt

Born in the Austrian Empire in 1862, Klimt was known for his symbolist works, many portraying the female body. He became successful towards the very beginning of the 20th century. He passed in 1918, but his remaining paintings have been auctioned off for tens of millions of dollars. One had even surpassed the selling price of one of Picasso's pieces at the time.

Edvard Munch

Even if you don't know Munch by name, you probably have seen his painting titled "The Scream" picturing a figure with their hands laid flat over their ears. Munch was born in Norway in 1863, and derived more of his artistic expression from his personal struggles than that of other famous Symbolism artists at the time. He followed the philosophy of "I do not believe in the art which is not the compulsive result of Man's urge to open his heart."

Gustave Moreau

Moreau was born in 1826 in Paris, France. He's widely considered the forerunner of Symbolism that helped the movement gain traction. His pieces mainly surround mythological and religious subjects. The subject matter was often two figures locking eyes, usually a man and a woman. His paintings were very elegant and divine in nature.
